MODULE 01 — WHY IT EXISTS

QR vs. the barcode

FIELD NOTE — 1994

Back in the early 1990s, Japanese car manufacturer Toyota had a problem. Their factories relied on traditional barcodes to track parts on the assembly line, but a single barcode could only hold about 20 characters — barely enough for a part number. Workers needed several barcodes stuck side by side just to describe one component, and scanning them one at a time slowed everything down.

In 1994, an engineer named Masahiro Hara at Denso Wave (a Toyota subsidiary) led the team that solved this by inventing the QR code. Instead of a single line of black-and-white stripes, they built a two-dimensional grid — meaning it could store information both horizontally and vertically.

TRADITIONAL BARCODE (1D)

Packed strictly into the width of the stripes; read along a single horizontal line. Capped at 20–25 characters. Requires laser-precise linear alignment.

VS

QR CODE (2D)

Packed into both width and height of the grid. Unlocks massive structural capacity and permits omnidirectional 360° scanning instantly.

MODULE 02 — HOW YOUR PHONE FINDS IT

Anatomy of the grid

Look closely at any QR code and you'll spot three identical square patterns in three of its four corners — a black square inside a white ring inside a black square. These are Finder Patterns, and they're what let your phone lock onto the code instantly, even in a cluttered photo. Tap a tag to inspect each structural piece.

Every square is a bit

READING METHOD

The software scans along a tight, rigid zigzag reading pattern (weaving up and down in two-column strips), parsing bytes through four advanced algorithmic configurations:

  • NUMERIC Exclusively maps numerical values for dense structural compression.
  • ALPHANUMERIC Translates blended matrices of text strings and numerical characters.
  • BYTE Universal internet standard converting full raw text, intricate URLs, and scripts.
  • KANJI Native double-byte mode mapping Japanese glyphs in honor of Denso Wave.
